These pursuits have taught me that true expertise in not a matter of inspiration but consistency.

Mentorship
Coaching, translating, and helping others improve
+

One of the most rewarding parts of my journey has been building relationships with mentors who have trusted me with meaningful work while helping me grow both personally and professionally. Their guidance has shaped how I approach investing, problem-solving, and continuous learning.

Growing up in a West African household and helping my family with their fish markets and investment firm in Togo taught me the importance of mentorship and community.

Whether pulling in a fishing net or building a career, I learned early that asking for help is not a weakness. It is actually often the fastest way to grow.

Today, I actively seek guidance from mentors across finance, consulting, law, entrepreneurship, and academia, and every major milestone in my career, I can confidently say has been strengthened by their advice.

Mentorship, however, is a two-way street. I've had the opportunity to coach track and field athletes at my former high school, helping students develop the technical fundamentals of throwing.

Coaching reinforced something I strongly believe and that is, unless you can explain a concept clearly to someone else, and watch them apply it successfully, you don't truly understand it yourself.

When friends ask me how I've been able to accomplish certain goals or secure different opportunities, my answer is almost always the same: seek mentorship. Hard work is essential, but learning from someone who has already walked the path y ou're trying to follow can dramatically accelerate your growth while helping you avoid mistakes they've already made. Because of this, mentorship is one of the first things I evaluate before joining any organization. I always ask myself, "What does the mentorship culture look like?" The opportunity to learn from experienced professionals while contributing to a collaborative team is one of the most important factors I consider before joining any organization. My goal is to continue paying that investment forward by becoming the kind of mentor others have been for me.

Selected Work

How I've Learned to Evaluate Businesses.

Private Capital
Capital raising • Investor materials • Due Dilligence

Supported SPV capital raises, investor communication, diligence coordination, and investment materials across sports, real estate, and emerging market opportunities.

Private equity gave me my first real look at how capital moves and how investment decisions are actually made. Before entering the industry as an apprentice, I assumed investing was primarily about building financial models and forecasting returns. While those skills are certainly important, I quickly learned that the strongest investment decisions begin long before a model is built. Through supporting capital raises, preparing investor materials, coordinating due diligence, and communicating with founders and investors, I saw how every investment opportunity tells a story. Financial performance matters, but so do management quality, incentives, execution risk, competitive positioning, and timing. Investors are ultimately placing confidence in people and their ability to execute over many years. Working across sports, commercial real estate, healthcare, and emerging market opportunities also reinforced that while industries may differ, the questions investors ask remain remarkably consistent: Can this business create lasting value? Does management have a durable advantage? And why is this opportunity compelling today? That experience transformed the way I evaluate businesses. I no longer look at financial statements in isolation, I try to understand the business behind the numbers.

Private Equity
Invesment Memoranda • Business Analysis • Valuation

Evaluated healthcare a cquisition targets and built investment memoranda and research projects connecting market structure, operations, policy, and long-term value creation.

My experience in healthcare private equity taught me how to think beyond financial statements and evaluate businesses the way investors do. Rather than simply reviewing historical performance, I learned to assess management teams, operational efficiency, competitive positioning, industry dynamics, and the factors that ultimately determine whether a company is capable of creating long-term value. A significant portion of my work involved preparing investment memoranda for physician practice acquisition opportunities across California, with businesses ranging from $5–10 million in EBITDA. Each memorandum required synthesizing financial analysis, management assessments, operational diligence, industry dynamics, valuation, and investment risks into a clear investment thesis for senior decision-makers. To date, I have contributed to investment memoranda supporting approximately five successful acquisitions, representing nearly $45 million of aggregate EBITDA across healthcare businesses. Every memorandum required distilling weeks of diligence into a concise recommendation that helped guide investment decisions throughout the acquisition process. One of the biggest lessons I took away from this experience is that great investments are rarely driven by numbers alone. Financial performance tells you where a business has been, but understanding management quality, succession planning, physician retention, reimbursement dynamics, referral relationships, customer concentration, and operational execution helps determine where it can go next. Working with physician practices also exposed me to the unique challenges of healthcare investing. A practice may have strong financial performance today, but long-term value depends on whether physicians are approaching retirement, whether there is a clear succession plan, how concentrated revenue is among providers or referral sources, and whether the practice can continue growing after an acquisition. Evaluating these operational and strategic considerations taught me that the quality of a business often lies beyond what can be captured in a financial model. Healthcare private equity ultimately reinforced that investing requires balancing quantitative analysis with qualitative judgment. Two companies may have similar financial profiles, yet the better investment is often the one with stronger leadership, a more durable competitive position, and a clearer path to sustainable long-term value creation.

Consulting
Strategy • SEO • Competitive Benchmarking •Market entry

Developed business plans, competitive benchmarking, alumni retention initiatives,customer acquisition recommendations, and international market entry analysis.

Consulting challenged me to think differently. Unlike investment research, where the objective is often to evaluate opportunities, consulting required me to develop practical recommendations that organizations could actually implement. Across projects involving business planning, SEO strategy, competitive benchmarking, alumni engagement, customer acquisition, and international market entry, I learned how to move beyond identifying problems and focus on building actionable solutions. One of the lessons I enjoyed most was recognizing that great ideas are only valuable if they can be communicated clearly. Whether presenting strategic recommendations to nonprofit organizations, benchmarking competitors for an international client, or helping businesses think through customer acquisition opportunities, I realized that effective communication is just as important as analytical ability. Consulting strengthened my ability to simplify complex information, work collaboratively with diverse teams, and translate research into strategies that create measurable impact.
